From Graduate Engineer Trainee to Lead Engineer II - a personal account of growth, grit, and gratitude at John Deere.

✦ 8 Years at John Deere ✦ 5 Roles Across the Ladder ✦ Written for My Team

This is the most asked question of all time - at least for anyone who has spent a significant stretch of their career here: "How has it been? Is it worth it? What do you get out of staying?"

Let me answer that honestly. You are sitting inside one of the world's safest, most nurturing professional environments - and that changes everything. This is my story of eight years inside that environment. Pull up a chair.

01

Chapter One · Where it all began

Graduate Engineer Trainee

Naive. Fresh. Eager. Completely unbothered by which technology I was being thrown into - just ready to jump into the deep end and figure it out on the way down. That was me on Day One. (And honestly? Still me. Just with better instincts now.)

That first year as a Graduated Engineer Trainee was about absorbing everything - the culture, the codebase, the people, the rhythm of how things move inside a large organisation. It was overwhelming in the best possible way. I didn't overthink it. I just showed up, stayed curious, and let the work teach me.

"The secret of getting ahead is getting started."

- Mark Twain

After a year of building foundations, I transitioned into Software Engineer II - and the real game began.

Chapter Two · The turning point

Software Engineer II

If the first chapter was about surviving, this one was about understanding yourself. Software Engineer II was technically an intermediate role before Senior Engineer - but it was anything but ordinary for me.

This phase cracked me open in the best way. I started to recognise where I genuinely thrived, where I needed more work, and what kind of engineer - and person - I wanted to become. Challenging? Absolutely. Interesting? Without question.

But perhaps the most underrated thing I did during this time was invest in people. Networking, building friendships, creating genuine connections - these weren't career tactics. They were life decisions. And they paid dividends I am still cashing today.

"Your network is your net worth - but only if you build it with sincerity, not strategy."

- A lesson learned the hard way

Chapter Three · The storm & the silver lining

Senior Software Engineer I

By this point, I had worked with a wide plethora of people, technologies, and problem spaces. It was time to step up - and the world had other plans too. This was the era of COVID, and with it came the company's first major global workforce restructuring: the SIRD - Smart Industrial Operating Model.

There was stress. There were worries. Projects got scrapped. Teams reshuffled. I was asked to move - again. Getting uprooted mid-project is taxing in ways that are hard to articulate unless you've lived it. But looking back, it was a Blessing in Disguise.

Because it was during this turbulence that I first stepped fully onto the Cloud. Before that I'd been experimenting - machine learning scripts, model evaluation pipelines - but this was the moment everything clicked. The Cloud became my strongest foundation. My sharpest toolkit. My peak era of networking and expanding scope. If Software Engineer II was where I found myself, Senior Engineer I was where I built myself.

"Sometimes the worst seasons of our career become the richest soil for growth. What feels like loss is often re-direction."

- Perspective earned through experience

Chapter Four · The golden era

Lead Engineer I

If I had to pick one word for this phase, it would be: golden.

This time period shaped me in more ways than I can fully enumerate here. Opportunities came in waves - meeting influential people, forging meaningful corporate connections, leading cross-collaboration projects, travelling, stepping into new roles, carrying real ownership, and learning at a pace that felt almost unfair.

The presentations. The 1-on-1 sessions. The hiring conversations. All of it added layers of capability I hadn't expected to develop so quickly. Lead Engineer I was when I stopped waiting to be handed things and started creating them.

"Leadership is not about being in charge. It is about taking care of those in your charge - and then making sure the work actually gets done."

- Simon Sinek (with a practical addendum)

Chapter Five · The full picture

Lead Engineer II → BRT

From Green & Yellow to Blue & White (pun intended), but as an org, this was the chapter that brought everything together.

Lead Engineer II taught me something none of the previous roles could: that no individual is greater than the team. The enterprise doesn't move on one person's brilliance - it moves on collective trust, shared language, and teams that genuinely want each other to succeed.

This phase was marked by deep stakeholder relationships, international travel, bonding with overseas and on-premise teams, and a level of cross-functional involvement that made every day feel consequential. Tech, non-tech, extracurricular - everything was firing at once, and it was exhilarating.

I curated my project management skills. I deepened my enterprise visibility. I gave more, learned more, and connected more than in any previous chapter. It was so good, I genuinely didn't want it to end - and that feeling, that bittersweet "I wish this wasn't over," is itself the greatest endorsement I can give this organisation.

And then came the transition to BRT - Blue River Technology. A move that felt like the natural next step: taking everything I'd built - the cloud expertise, the leadership instincts, the cross-functional muscle - and channelling it into a team pushing the edge of autonomous agriculture. Same Deere DNA, entirely new canvas. It was the perfect evolution.

"Alone we can do so little; together we can do so much."

- Helen Keller

A Letter to the Team I'm Leaving Behind

Eight years. Five roles. Countless late nights, project pivots, team lunches, offsite trips, late-night Teams/Slack messages, and moments of genuine pride. This place grew me in ways I couldn't have scripted, and I want you to know that this organisation absolutely serves its purpose - you just have to stay active, stay curious, and stay present enough to receive it.

But here's the thing I most want to leave you with - and please, please take this seriously:

"Work like a human being who loves their craft - not like a machine trying to prove something. Go out. Have fun. Laugh with your team. Log off sometimes. The best work you'll ever do comes from a person who is genuinely alive, not from someone who is burning themselves to the ground."

 ✦  This is the USP of this team. Protect it  ✦ 

You are in a safe environment. That's rare. Use that safety to take creative risks, build real relationships, explore unfamiliar technology, and grow at your own pace without shame. Don't waste that privilege chasing urgency that doesn't exist.

The connections you make here - the friendships, the mentors, the quiet champions who advocate for you in rooms you'll never be in - those will follow you everywhere. Invest in people as much as you invest in code.

And when the hard phases come - the project cancellations, the org restructures, the moments where everything feels uncertain - remember that those chapters often end up being the most formative ones of all. Sit inside the discomfort. Let it teach you.

It has been an absolute privilege. I grew here. I laughed here. I struggled here. And I leave here deeply grateful.

With every ounce of appreciation - thank you for eight extraordinary years. Now go do something brilliant. 🌱

- Your teammate, always
Shubham
